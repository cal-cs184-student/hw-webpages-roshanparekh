Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

The ray generation pipeline starts by taking 2D pixel coordinates from the image and converting them into a 3D ray in world space. First, we mapped the normalized (x, y) image coordinates to the virtual camera sensor, which sits on the Z=-1 plane in camera space. We created a ray originating at the camera's local origin (0,0,0) and pointing towards that specific calculated point on the sensor. Since this ray is still in camera space, we multiplied the direction vector by the camera-to-world rotation matrix (c2w), translated the origin to the camera's world position, and then normalized the final direction vector so it's ready to be shot into the scene. We also made sure to initialize the ray's min_t and max_t bounds using the camera's near and far clipping planes.

Once the rays are generated, they are cast into the scene to see what geometry they hit. For the triangle intersection part, we implemented the Moller-Trumbore algorithm. This method is great because it lets you calculate both the intersection distance (t) and the barycentric coordinates of the hit point simultaneously, without needing to explicitly compute the plane equation first. By checking if the resulting barycentric coordinates are valid (all between 0 and 1, and summing to 1), we can quickly confirm whether the ray actually pierced the inside of the triangle or missed it.

Whenever a ray hits a valid point on a primitive (either a triangle or a sphere), we check if the intersection distance (t) falls strictly within the ray's current min_t and max_t bounds. If it does, that means it is the closest object the ray has intersected so far. We then update the ray's max_t to this new distance so the renderer knows to ignore anything hidden behind it. Finally, we populate an Intersection struct with the surface normal and the material BSDF. For triangles, we calculate the exact surface normal at the hit point by using the barycentric coordinates to interpolate the three vertex normals.

Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy

For the bounding box intersection, we implemented the ray and axis-aligned box intersection method from lecture. An axis-aligned bounding box is essentially just the space where three pairs of parallel planes overlap. To check if a ray hits the box, we calculated the t-values for when the ray crosses the min and max planes of the X, Y, and Z axes. If the ray happens to be traveling in a negative direction along any axis, we just swapped the min and max t-values so the smaller number is always the entry point and the larger is the exit. Once we had the entry and exit times for all three axes, we found the overall entry time into the 3D box by taking the maximum of the three individual entry times. We found the overall exit time by taking the minimum of the three exit times. As long as the final entry time was less than or equal to the exit time, and that interval overlapped with the ray's valid min_t and max_t bounds, we considered it a valid intersection. From there, we just updated the t0 and t1 values back out for the BVH traversal to use.

We compared rendering times for a few moderately complex scenes, such as the beetle and the teapot, to evaluate the impact of our BVH implementation. Without BVH acceleration, the beetle scene took about 14.7 seconds to render and the teapot took roughly 4.6 seconds, largely because the naive approach tests every single ray against every primitive in the scene. This resulted in hundreds or even over a thousand intersection tests per single ray. After implementing the BVH, the rendering times for both of these scenes dropped dramatically down to around 0.03 seconds. This massive improvement happens because the bounding volume hierarchy allows the renderer to quickly skip over large groups of geometry that a ray is guaranteed to miss, effectively reducing the intersection complexity from linear to logarithmic and letting us render complex meshes in just a fraction of a second.

Part 3: Direct Illumination

For our first direct lighting function, estimate_direct_lighting_hemisphere, we set up a coordinate system at the hit point and used a uniform sampler to pick random directions in the hemisphere above the surface. We converted these directions into world space so we could cast a shadow ray to see if we hit a light. If the ray hit an object with emission, we took that radiance and multiplied it by the BSDF and a cosine term, then divided by the 1/(2pi) PDF. After looping through all our samples, we averaged the result to get the total light bouncing back to the camera.

In the second version, estimate_direct_lighting_importance, we changed things up to sample the light sources directly instead of just guessing random directions. We iterated through every light in the scene and used a sampling function to get the direction, distance, and PDF for each one. We cast a shadow ray toward each light, but we made sure to set the max_t so we only checked for objects blocking the path between the surface and the light source. If the path was clear, we added the light's contribution using the reflection equation. This approach is much better because it lets us handle point lights and gets rid of a lot of the graininess we saw in the hemisphere version.

Part 4: Global Illumination

For our indirect lighting function (at_least_one_bounce_radiance), we start by calculating the one-bounce direct lighting at the current intersection point. If our ray depth is exactly the max depth, or if we are accumulating bounces, we add this direct lighting to our total output. Then, we check our base case: if the ray's depth has reached 1, we stop recursing and just return the light we have so far.

If we haven't hit the base case, we use Russian Roulette to decide if the ray should keep bouncing. We set a termination probability of 0.3 (meaning a continuation probability of 0.7). If max_ray_depth is greater than 1, we always guarantee at least one indirect bounce, but for any bounces after that, we flip a coin using our continuation probability. If the coin flip says to continue, we sample the BSDF at the hit point to get a new incoming direction and its PDF. We convert that direction to world space, create a new bounce ray with its depth decreased by 1, and offset its origin by EPS_F to avoid self-intersection.

Finally, we trace this new bounce ray into the scene. If it hits something, we recursively call at_least_one_bounce_radiance on that new intersection. We take the returned light and apply the rendering equation: we multiply by the BSDF evaluation and the cosine of the angle, then divide by the PDF. To keep our estimator unbiased because of Russian Roulette, we also divide the final amount by our continuation probability (0.7) before adding it to our total output.

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling

Adaptive sampling is an optimization that allows us to dynamically adjust the number of rays traced per pixel. Rather than uniformly casting a maximum number of rays (like 2048) across the entire scene—which is computationally expensive. We use statistical convergence to stop sampling early. Simpler regions, like flat walls, converge quickly and smoothly. More complex areas, such as soft shadows, have higher variance and require significantly more samples to eliminate noise.

Implementation:
In raytrace_pixel, we implemented this by tracking the total illuminance and the squared illuminance as the sampling loop runs. Every time the number of samples reaches a multiple of samplesPerBatch, we calculate the mean and standard deviation of the samples collected so far. We then use these statistics to compute the 95% confidence interval, I. If I ≤ maxTolerance · μ, we can safely assume the pixel's lighting has stabilized and break out of the loop to save computation time. Finally, we record the actual number of samples taken into the sampleCountBuffer so the program can generate the rate images.
